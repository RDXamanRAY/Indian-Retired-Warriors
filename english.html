ENGLISH ELECTRIC CANBERRA
RETURN

Service History


  • Entry into service : The Canberra entered IAF service in 1957. India acquired approximately 107 Canberras,

    and the aircraft quickly became the backbone of India’s bomber and reconnaissance fleet.


  • Mjaor Operation : The Canberra saw extensive combat service during the 965 Indo-Pak War, 1971 Indo-Pak War, and Liberation of Goa in 1961.

    It played crucial roles in strategic bombing, reconnaissance, and intelligence gathering.


  • Ongoing Service: The Canberra was retired in 2007 after 50 years of service, making it one of the longest-serving

    aircraft in the IAF’s history. Its roles were gradually replaced by more advanced aircraft, including the Jaguar and Su-30MKI.


Technical Specifications


  • Weight : Approximately 10 tons (empty weight).

  • Engine : Powered by two Rolls-Royce Avon Mk 109 turbojet engines, each generating 7,400 pounds of thrust.

  • Speed : Capable of speeds up to 885 km/h (Mach 0.7).

  • Armament :
  • Primary: The Canberra was generally unarmed in IAF service and used primarily for bombing and reconnaissance.
  • External Hardpoints: Capable of carrying up to 4,500 kg of bombs, including conventional and incendiary bombs. Some variants were also adapted for electronic warfare.

  • Notable Feature : The Canberra’s high-altitude capability and large payload made it ideal for bombing and reconnaissance missions.

  • Range : Operational range of approximately 2,700 km, with the potential for extended missions using drop tanks.

  • Crew : Typically a two-man crew: pilot and navigator.

Historical Significance


The Canberra was instrumental in establishing India’s strategic bombing and reconnaissance capabilities, providing a substantial boost to India’s defense infrastructure. Its versatility allowed it to conduct a wide range of missions, from high-altitude bombing to intelligence gathering, which proved especially valuable during both wartime and peacetime. The Canberra’s long service life reflects its durability, operational success, and adaptability to evolving mission requirements.

Notable Missions


  • Liberation of Goa (1961):The Canberra was used for bombing Portuguese positions, supporting ground troops and assisting in the successful liberation of Goa.

  • 1965 Indo-Pak War:The Canberra conducted strategic bombing missions and reconnaissance over enemy positions, airfields, and supply routes, proving essential in the IAF’s campaign.

  • 1971 Indo-Pak War:The Canberra played a critical role in bombing missions and night raids over enemy territory, targeting infrastructure and military installations. It also performed electronic warfare roles to disrupt enemy communications.

  • Reconnaissance Missions:The Canberra’s high-altitude, long-range reconnaissance capabilities were frequently used to monitor enemy movement and gather intelligence, particularly along India’s borders.
